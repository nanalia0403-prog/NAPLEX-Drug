CBC & ANC: Focus on infection risk, anemia pattern, platelet safety, and medication causes.

ANC Calculator

ANC = WBC × (% neutrophils + bands) ÷ 100Severe neutropenia: ANC <500 cells/mm³.

High-Yield CBC Patterns

WBC / ANC ↓Chemotherapy, clozapine, carbamazepine, methimazole → infection precautions; fever with ANC <500 is an emergency.
Hemoglobin ↓Bleeding, iron deficiency, CKD, marrow suppression. ESA therapy in CKD requires iron assessment.
Platelets ↓Heparin exposure + >50% fall suggests HIT; stop all heparin and use a non-heparin anticoagulant.
MCVLow: iron deficiency; high: B12/folate deficiency or selected drugs.
Liver panel: Do not interpret AST or ALT alone. Identify the pattern, synthetic function, symptoms, and medication exposure.

Pattern Recognition

AST/ALT disproportionately highHepatocellular injury: viral hepatitis, ischemia, acetaminophen, drug-induced liver injury.
ALP + bilirubin highCholestatic or obstructive pattern. Confirm hepatic source when appropriate.
Albumin ↓ / INR ↑Reduced hepatic synthetic function; can suggest advanced or acute severe disease.
AST:ALT >2Classic clue for alcohol-associated liver injury, but not diagnostic alone.

Medication Connections

AcetaminophenVery high aminotransferases; use the nomogram for eligible acute ingestions and give N-acetylcysteine when indicated.
StatinsBaseline liver evaluation; routine repeated LFT monitoring is not required without symptoms or concern.
Valproate / isoniazid / methotrexateHigh-yield hepatotoxic drugs requiring clinical and laboratory monitoring.
Coagulation: Match the anticoagulant to the correct monitoring test and reversal strategy.

Monitoring Map

Warfarin → INRMost indications target INR 2–3. Major bleeding: 4-factor PCC + IV phytonadione.
Unfractionated heparin → aPTT or anti-XaProtamine reverses heparin; calculate from the amount received recently.
LMWH → usually no routine testAnti-Xa may be used in selected pregnancy, extreme body weight, or renal dysfunction scenarios.
DOACsNo routine INR monitoring. Use drug-specific timing, renal function, and reversal information.

HIT Pattern

TimingUsually 5–10 days after heparin exposure, or sooner with recent exposure.
Platelet changeA fall of >50% is more important than the absolute count.
ActionStop every heparin source; start a non-heparin anticoagulant when appropriate. Do not wait for thrombosis.
Therapeutic Drug Monitoring: Memorize the sample timing, target concept, toxicity clue, and organ function that changes clearance.
VancomycinAUC-guided monitoring for serious MRSA infections; monitor renal function.
Lithium12-hour trough; toxicity risk rises with dehydration, thiazides, ACEI/ARB, and NSAIDs.
DigoxinDraw at least 6–8 hours after a dose; interpret with renal function and electrolytes.
PhenytoinCorrect total level for low albumin or use a free level; nonlinear kinetics.
ValproateMonitor level, CBC/platelets, and liver function; consider ammonia with encephalopathy.
CarbamazepineLevel, CBC, sodium, and liver function; autoinduction and many interactions.
AminoglycosidesPeak relates to efficacy; trough relates to toxicity. Use regimen-specific goals.
Tacrolimus / CyclosporineTrough concentrations, renal function, potassium, magnesium, glucose, and interactions.
Acid–Base: Use the same order every time: pH → primary process → expected compensation → anion gap → clinical cause.

Anion Gap Calculator

AG = Na − (Cl + HCO₃⁻)Common uncorrected study range is approximately 8–12 mEq/L.

Decision Path

pH <7.35Acidemia. Low HCO₃⁻ suggests metabolic acidosis; high PaCO₂ suggests respiratory acidosis.
pH >7.45Alkalemia. High HCO₃⁻ suggests metabolic alkalosis; low PaCO₂ suggests respiratory alkalosis.
High AG acidosisLactate, ketoacidosis, renal failure, and selected toxins/drugs.
Normal AG acidosisDiarrhea, renal tubular acidosis, acetazolamide, or saline-related hyperchloremia.
Winter's formulaExpected PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 ±2.
ECG & Electrolytes: The exam often gives one tracing clue and asks for the electrolyte, drug, or immediate action.

Electrolyte Clues

K⁺ ↑Peaked T waves → PR prolongation → wide QRS → sine wave. Stabilize the myocardium with IV calcium when ECG changes are present.
K⁺ ↓Flattened T waves, ST depression, U waves, and increased digoxin toxicity risk.
Mg²⁺ ↓Torsades risk. IV magnesium is the classic treatment for torsades.
Ca²⁺ ↓ / ↑Low calcium prolongs QT; high calcium shortens QT.

Drug Clues

QT prolongationMacrolides, fluoroquinolones, antipsychotics, methadone, and class IA/III antiarrhythmics are common exam examples.
DigoxinScooped ST appearance can occur; toxicity causes multiple arrhythmias and GI/visual symptoms.
WPW + atrial fibrillationAvoid isolated AV-nodal blockers; use an appropriate pathway-directed strategy.
Endocrine & Lipids: Memorize diagnostic cutoffs and the values that trigger urgent risk management.

Diabetes & Thyroid

A1C<5.7% normal; 5.7–6.4% prediabetes; ≥6.5% diabetes when diagnostic criteria are met.
Fasting glucose100–125 mg/dL prediabetes; ≥126 mg/dL diabetes when confirmed as appropriate.
TSH ↑ + free T4 ↓Primary hypothyroidism. TSH ↓ + free T4 ↑ suggests primary hyperthyroidism.
Calcium + PTHInterpret together; albumin and kidney function can change the clinical meaning.

Lipids

Triglycerides ≥500 mg/dLPancreatitis risk becomes a major treatment concern.
LDL-CTreatment intensity is driven by ASCVD risk, clinical ASCVD, diabetes, and severe hypercholesterolemia.
Statin safetyCheck CK for significant muscle symptoms, not as routine screening in every patient.
